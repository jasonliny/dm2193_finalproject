Platform vs. Publisher

The distinction between a service being a platform or publiser has major legal implications, mostly related to Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act. It determines whether or not one can be held responsible for hosting certain content. Before Section 230, similar laws were present for physical goods, a common example being stores that sell physical materials such as books. These establishements were considered distributers and were not held strictly liable for all the content they sold, and this is what many online services today (most notably social media companies) want to be labeled as.

Claims

Platform

  • Platforms should be "neutral" meaning they allow and treat all types of content equally.
  • As a result, moderation should be limited.
  • The platform is not responsible for content posted.

Publisher

  • Publishers have curated content.
  • As a result, an active role is played in choosing what content is should or should not be published.
  • The publisher is responsible for content that is posted.

Motivation

Platform

  • Platforms say they are not liable for what is posted on their platform.
  • They can then forgo moderating posts.

Publisher

  • Publishers are held liable for the content published.
  • They need people to review content and determine if it should be published.

The Problem

  • "Platforms" are attempting to rid themselves of any responsibility over content posted by users.
  • However, once these platforms reach such a large scale, the impact of harmful content is substantially more significant.
  • Content that rouses negative emotions has been shown to have higher engagement, which is then promoted and put at the top of feeds.
  • Platforms are hypocritial, as they already selectively exercise their role as a publisher.

A Way Forward

  • Create a clear and transparent procedure for determining whether content should stay posted.
  • Having a set of guidelines for content restriction helps prevent arbitrary moderation decisions being made that can be abused.
  • Get rid of algorithmic feeds.
  • Stop promoting engaging content, which incentivizes harmful content and contributes to the creation of "filter bubbles"
